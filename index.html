Reading 1: Living with Algorithms

Technology & AI

PART A: Comprehension Questions (Paraphrased)

1. What is the main argument the writer is making about Artificial Intelligence in paragraph A?

The writer believes that by letting AI make everyday choices for us, we are slowly losing our personal freedom and our ability to think independently, even though it seems convenient.

2. How does the writer support their argument with personal experience in paragraph B?

The author explains that when they used an AI tool to write a story, their ideas became boring and unoriginal because the machine was doing all the hard work instead of them.

3. Compare the tech companies' view of AI convenience vs. the writer's view of it.

While technology companies see AI as a perfect tool to make life easier by matching our preferences, the writer argues that this convenience is actually harmful because it takes away our personal control.

4. What does the phrase “echo chambers” really mean in the context of paragraph C?

In this context, it describes a situation where people only see opinions and information that match their own beliefs, which traps them in a digital bubble and prevents them from learning different viewpoints.

5. According to the text, what important skill must be taught in schools to solve this problem?

Schools need to teach digital literacy so that students learn how to analyze the technology they use, actively search for different opinions, and maintain their independent thinking skills.

Reading 2: The True Cost of Cheap Trends

Environment & Consumerism

PART A: Comprehension Questions (Paraphrased)

1. What is the main argument the writer makes about the fast fashion business model?

The author argues that although fast fashion provides cheap and trendy clothes, it causes massive damage to the environment through huge carbon emissions and textile waste.

2. How does the writer support their argument with personal experience in paragraph B?

The writer shares their experience as a journalist visiting factories in developing countries, where they personally met young women working extremely long hours in dangerous conditions for very little money.

3. Compare the concept of "fast fashion" with the "slow fashion" movement suggested by the writer.

Fast fashion focuses on buying cheap, low-quality clothes that are thrown away quickly, whereas slow fashion encourages buying high-quality or second-hand items and repairing them so they last a long time.

4. How does social media psychologically affect young consumers' buying habits?

Social media creates strong pressure on young people by using influencers to promote constantly changing trends, making teenagers feel they must keep buying new clothes to feel valuable and stay fashionable.

5. According to the text, what specific actions can consumers take to solve this problem?

Consumers can help by choosing well-made clothes that last longer, shopping at second-hand stores, and fixing damaged items instead of simply throwing them in the trash.

Reading 3: The Billionaire Space Race

Space & Privatization

PART A: Comprehension Questions (Paraphrased)

1. What is the main argument the writer makes regarding how space exploration has changed?

The writer points out that space exploration is no longer controlled by national governments for scientific goals; instead, it has become a private business run by billionaires for profit.

2. How does the writer support their argument with personal experience in paragraph B?

The author mentions that they recently watched a private company successfully reuse a rocket to deliver supplies, which proves that commercial businesses can innovate faster than government agencies.

3. Compare how space programs were funded in the past versus how they operate today.

In the past, space programs were paid for by ordinary citizens' taxes and driven by competition between countries, but today they are funded and operated by wealthy business owners.

4. What does the phrase “legal wild west” really mean in paragraph C?

The phrase means that space is currently an uncontrolled area with no clear international laws or rules, meaning private companies might do whatever they want without facing legal consequences.

5. According to the text, what must happen to ensure space doesn't become a "corporate playground"?

To prevent billionaires from taking over space, countries must create new international agreements that control these private companies and protect space as a shared resource for everyone.

Speaking Exam Study Guide

Units 4 & 5 (Max 1 Minute Output)

PART 1: The B2 Speaking Toolkit

Use these structures to ensure your 45-60 second speech flows logically and scores highly in discourse management.

💡 Expressing Opinion

  • "I firmly believe that..." / "I am convinced that..."
  • "From my perspective..." / "As far as I am concerned..."
  • "I reckon that..." / "It seems to me that..."

🏗️ Supporting Arguments

  • "The primary reason for this is..."
  • "This is mainly because..."
  • "Furthermore..." / "Moreover..."

🔍 Exemplifying

  • "A prime example of this would be..."
  • "To illustrate this point, consider..."
  • "For instance, if we look at..."

⚖️ Concession & Speculation

  • "While it is true that [X], I still believe..."
  • "Despite the obvious benefits of..."
  • "They must have..." / "It might be..."

PART 2: Power Vocabulary Bank

Unit 5: Artifact, excavation, preservation, heritage, legacy, acquisition, loot, consequences, justification, repatriate, admit, blame, obsess, defensive.

PART 3: Exam Practice & Sample Answers

Below are two sample responses written at a B1+ level. They are approximately 110 words long, which takes exactly 45-60 seconds to speak out loud at a natural pace.

Highlight Legend: Giving Opinion Supporting Exemplifying Concession Speculation Target Vocabulary
Card 1: Museum Ethics & Tech

"If a museum can create a perfect, interactive 3D hologram of a stolen artifact, should they return the original to its home country and just display the hologram? Or is there a special 'legacy' and value in seeing the original, physical object? Justify your opinion."

"I firmly believe that museums should return stolen artifacts to their home countries. While it is true that seeing the original object has a special legacy and historical value, we cannot ignore the moral consequences of keeping items that were looted in the past. To illustrate this point, consider the Elgin Marbles. They belong to Greece's cultural heritage. Furthermore, with modern innovation, museums can easily create 3D holograms. These holograms must be amazing for visitors to interact with. Therefore, a museum could have the digital version to educate people, while the real item is repatriated. In conclusion, it seems to me that technology gives us the perfect justification to do the right thing."
Card 2: Life Skills & Competition

"When someone points out a mistake you made, do you naturally get defensive? What are some strategies you can use to stay calm, listen, and take responsibility instead of immediately shifting the blame?"

"From my perspective, it is very natural to get defensive when someone criticizes our work. This is mainly because we feel intense anxiety about our performance. For instance, if we look at a difficult team project at school, when a teacher points out a mistake, a student might be tempted to immediately shift the blame to others. However, I reckon that it is essential to admit our errors. Instead of getting angry, we should cooperate and listen carefully. Despite the obvious stress of criticism, it can act as a great catalyst for personal improvement. In short, we must have an open mind so we don't underperform in the future."

Essay Writing Tips

The Standard 4-Paragraph Structure

PART 1: The Essay Skeleton

A successful exam essay needs a clear, predictable structure. Use the boxes below to guide the layout of your paragraphs.

1️⃣ The Introduction

Sets the stage and states your opinion.

  • The Hook: Start with a general statement about the topic.
  • The Background: Briefly explain the two sides of the debate.
  • The Thesis Statement: Clearly state your main opinion (e.g., "This essay will argue that...").

2️⃣ Body Paragraphs (T.E.E.L.)

Build 2 body paragraphs using this method:

  • Topic Sentence: State the main idea of the paragraph.
  • Explanation: Explain *why* you think this is true.
  • Evidence/Example: Give a real-world example.
  • Link: A short concluding sentence that links back to your thesis.

🔗 Essential Linking Words

Use these at the start of your sentences:

  • Adding ideas: Furthermore, Moreover, In addition.
  • Contrasting: However, On the other hand, Nevertheless.
  • Showing results: Therefore, Consequently, As a result.

🏁 The Conclusion

Wrap up without adding new information.

  • Restate Thesis: State your main opinion again using *different* words.
  • Summarize: Briefly remind the reader of your 2 main points.
  • Final Thought: End with a strong statement, a warning, or a hope for the future.

PART 2: Exam Pro-Tips

💡 Avoid informal language: Do not use contractions (e.g., use "do not" instead of "don't"). Avoid slang and overly emotional words.

💡 Plan before you write: Spend the first 5 minutes of your exam writing a quick outline. Knowing your Topic Sentences before you start writing will prevent you from getting stuck.

💡 Use a counter-argument: To get the highest marks, start your second body paragraph by acknowledging the opposite opinion before disproving it (e.g., "While some people argue that [Opposite Opinion], they fail to consider that...").
